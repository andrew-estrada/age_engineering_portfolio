FSAE

What is Formula SAE?

Formula SAE (FSAE) is a student-run project that involves designing, building, testing, and racing a small open wheel race car. The FSAE competition occurs yearly, and students compete against collegiate teams from around the country in a variety of static and dynamic competition events.  FSAE is a once-in-a-lifetime, hands-on engineering experience that gives students like me an opportunity to apply their knowledge into practice.

Front Pushrod Suspension- Rocker Arms

As a sophomore, I co-founded a novel Formula SAE club at NAU with the ultimate goal of building a car for my senior capstone project. Through a lot of hard work and relentless sponsorship campaigns, this vision became a reality. I am  focused on the suspension system, and I was specifically tasked with designing a cost-effective and innovative front pushrod suspension.

Design Goals

  • Ease Of Manufacture
  • Linear Motion Ratio
  • System Provides at Least 50mm of wheel travel (V.3.1.1)
  • Lightweight

Geometry Optimization

Simple Rocker Arm Geometry

A linear motion ratio was found to be desirable for the characteristics in a first FSAE car due to the predictable response and ease of tuning the damper. This makes the geometry of the rocker extremely important. The lengths of each side of the rocker as well as the angle between them as shown in the triangle below allows us to modify the dynamic response. Through iterative development and simulation in LOTUS Suspension Shark, I was able to create this linear response.

Analysis

To find a starting point for the static construction of the suspension system we first started by writing a load transfer script in MATLAB. Through analysis and referencing RCVD, it was determined that a 3G Bump scenario would be bind the suspension design. For the Static analysis ANSYS was used to simulate the load cases, and optimize the removal of excess material while maintaining the structural integrity.

Manufacturing

From the design's inception, manufacturability was a top priority, especially since I would be the machinist responsible for this part. The rocker was initially designed as a single-operation part that wouldn't require soft jaws, relying on tabs instead. However, during the programming phase, it became clear that the tabs were less than ideal for part retention due to the available stock. As a result, the design was adjusted to a straightforward two-operation process, including a few cosmetic fillets. Below are some pictures of the machining process. The rocker met all tolerance requirements and achieved a fantastic surface finish. The CAM for this part was completed within Mastercam.
