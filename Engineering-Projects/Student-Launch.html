NASA Student Launch

Whats Nasa Student Launch?

The NASA Student Launch Competition is an annual event that challenges student teams to design, construct, and launch high-powered rockets aimed at reaching specified altitude and payload objectives. This competition emphasizes the development of STEM skills by immersing participants in real-world aerospace engineering projects, including everything from design documentation to actual flight testing. It culminates in a launch at NASA’s Marshall Space Flight Center, providing invaluable hands-on experience in both engineering and collaboration. 

Our university earned a place in the competition after securing a win at the NASA First Nations Launch (FNL). We were tasked with the design and manufacture of a high power rocket reaching a target altitude between 4,000-5000 ft. carrying a payload which featured an automated camera system capable of imaging the immediate landing vicinity.

Avionics

The avionics and recovery system for our rocket were meticulously designed to ensure successful recovery and accurate data capture throughout each phase of descent. The avionics subsystem includes dual Featherweight Raven altimeters (Raven 3 and Raven 4) for redundancy in detecting critical flight events, such as apogee and parachute deployment. These altimeters, powered by dedicated LiPo batteries, trigger black powder charges to deploy parachutes at precise altitudes, reducing the risk of deployment failure. The recovery system uses a dual deployment strategy, beginning with the deployment of a Fruity Chutes 18-inch drogue chute at apogee, which is crucial for controlling descent speed early in the rocket's return. This drogue chute stabilizes the rocket while maintaining a descent speed around 70 mph—fast enough to minimize drift yet slow enough to ensure stability and control. At 600 feet AGL, the 72-inch Iris main chute is deployed to reduce the final descent speed for a safe landing, keeping the total descent time within the 90-second limit. A Featherweight GPS tracker provides real-time location tracking for efficient recovery, transmitting data at 915 MHz to a ground station. This integrated avionics and recovery design ensured a 100% recovery success rate over five test flights, highlighting its reliability and effectiveness.

As a member of the avionics team, I was responsible for the Featherweight GPS system and ensuring its functionality and integration. The GPS, housed within the avionics bay, integrates seamlessly with the ground station to provide real-time tracking, allowing us to maintain a reliable connection to the rocket during its descent and to locate it accurately upon landing. This GPS solution was selected for its lightweight design, battery life, and compatibility with our dual-deploy system, all critical in meeting mission requirements.

Manufacturing

From the design's inception, manufacturability was a top priority, especially since I would be the machinist responsible for this part. The rocker was initially designed as a single-operation part that wouldn't require soft jaws, relying on tabs instead. However, during the programming phase, it became clear that the tabs were less than ideal for part retention due to the available stock. As a result, the design was adjusted to a straightforward two-operation process, including a few cosmetic fillets. Below are some pictures of the machining process.
